## How Individuals Makes Choices Based on Their Budget constraint

- Consumers limited income
- Budget constraint
	- Represents all possible combinations of goods and services a customer can purchase within their limited income and given prices


### The Concept of Opportunity Cost

- Opportunity cost
	- Used to indicate what people must given up to obtain what they desire
	- The cost of one item is the lost opportunity to do or consume something else
	- Value of the next best alternative
- Every choice has an opportunity cost

#### Budget Constraints

$P$ = price
$Q$ = quantity

### Marginal Decision-Making and Diminishing Marginal Utility

- Marginal analysis
	- Examining the benefits and costs of choosing a little more or a little less of a good
- Utility
	- Subjective
	- The more of some good one consumes the more utility one obtains
	- The utility a person received from consuming the first unit of a good if typically more than the utility received form consuming later units
- Law of diminishing marginal utility
	- As a person received more of a good the additional utility from each additional unit of the good declines

### Sunk Costs
- Sunk costs
	- Costs that were incurred in the past and cannot be recovered, should not affect the current decision

## The Production Possibilities Frontier and Social Choices

- Production possibility frontier (PPF)
	- There is a limit to the quantity of goods and services it can produce


<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260401224226.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Society can choose any combination of the two goods on or inside the PPF
- Does not have enough resources to product outside the PPF
- Slope
	- Opportunity cost of PPF


**Difference between a budget constraint and a PPF**
- Budget constraint
	- Straight line
- PPF
	- Curved
	- Law of diminishing returns
	- No specific quantities

### The PPF and the Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost

- Consumption budget constraint
	- Determines the slope of the budget constraint
- Law of increasing opportunity cost
	- As production of a good or service increases, the marginal opportunity cost of producing it increases as well

### Productive Efficiency and Allocation Efficiency

- 2 types of efficiency shown by PPF
	- Productive efficiency
	- Allocative efficiency

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260401225140.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Productive efficency
	- Given the available inputs, it is impossible to produce more of one good without decreasing the quantity that is produced of another good
	- A, B, C, D, F
	- R is productively inefficiency
		- Possible to have more goods in education and healthcare sector
- Allocative efficiency
	- The combination of goods on the production possibility curve represents the combination that society most desires
	- Producers supply the quantity of each product that consumers demand

### Why Society Must Choose

- Situations that expand consumption of all goods
	- Improving efficiency and producing more of all goods
	- Improvements of technology can increase the level of production with given resources
- For both the government and market economy in the short term, increases in producing of one good mean offsetting decreases somewhere else

### The PPF and Comparative Advantage
- Countries have different opportunity costs of producing a specific good
	- Climates
	- Geography
	- Technology
	- Skills
