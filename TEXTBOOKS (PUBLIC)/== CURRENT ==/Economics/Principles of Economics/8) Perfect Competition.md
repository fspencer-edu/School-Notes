## How Perfectly Competitive Firms Make Output Decisions

- Perfect competition
	- Firms produce identical products
	- Buyers are available to but the product
	- Sellers and buyers have all relevant information to make rational decisions about buying/selling
	- Firms can enter and leave the market without restrictions

- Price taker
	- A perfectly competitive firm
	- Pressure of competing firms forces it to accept the prevailing equilibrium in the market
	- Agricultural markets

- In the long run, positive economic profits will attract competition as other firms enter the market
- Economic losses will cause firms to exit the market
- Competitive markets will attain long-run equilibrium when no new firms enter or leave the market
- Economic profits are close to zero

### Comparing Marginal Revenue and Marginal Costs

- Profit maximization level of output
	- Total revenue
	- Total cost
- Marginal revenue
	- Additional revenue gained from selling one more unit
	- Marginal revenue
	- Marginal cost
- Practical approach to maximizing profits means examining how changes in production affect marginal revenue and marginal cost
- A firm's demand curve is a horizontal line at market price level (elastic)
- Marginal revenue curve is the same as demand curve
	- Every time a consumer demands one more unit, the firm sells one more unit and revenue increases by exactly the same amount equal to the market price

### The Shutdown Point

- Shutting down can reduce variable costs to zero
- But in the short tun, fixed costs are already paid for
- Shutdown point
	- Intersection of the average variable cost curve and the marginal cost curve
	- The price below which the firm would lack enough revenue to cover its variable costs
	- Price above the shutdown point
		- Firm makes enough revenue to cover a portion of fixed costs

Price < min. average variable cost => shut down
Price > min. average variable cost => firm stays in business

### Short-Run Outcomes for Perfectly Competitive Firms

### Marginal Cost and the Firm's Supply Curve

- The marginal cost curve is identical to the firm's supply curve
- Firm checks the market prices, and its marginal cost to determine the quantity to product
- Price is greater than the min. AVC
- Marginal cost curve above the m. point on the AVC becomes the firm's supply curve
	- P = MC

## Entry and Exit Decisions in the Long Run

- Impossible to define the line between the short and long run
- In short run
	- Firms cannot change the usage of fixed inputs
- Long run
	- Firm can adjust all factors of production
- Entry
	- New firms enter the industry in response to increased industry profits
- Exit
	- Long-run process of reducing production in response to sustained pattern of losses


### How Entry and Exit Lead to Zero Profits in the Long Run

- A shift in supply for the market as a whole will affect the market price
- Entry and exit to and from the market are the driving forces
	- Pushes the price down to minimum average total costs so that all firms are earning a zero profit

- Long-run equilibrium
	- All firms earn zero economic profits
	- P = MR = MC, and P = AC
	- No incentive to leave or enter
- If the demand increases, the market increases, and the existing firms have a higher price, and can increased production where P = MR = MC

- Economic profits attract other firms to enter the market, and return to a equilibrium

- Short run losses will fade away by revering this process
	- Exit of many firms causes the market supply curve to move to the left
	- The market price starts to increase, and economic losses are decreased
- In the long run, this process of entry and exit will drive the price in perfectly competitive markets to the zero-profit point at the bottom of the AC curve
	- MC = AC

### The Long-Run Adjustment and Industry Types

- Constant-cost industry
	- As demand increases, and cost of production stays the same
	- Supply curve is elastic
	- Demand for corn in ethanol
- Increasing cost industry
	- As demand increases, the cost of production increases
	- Limited inputs, such as skilled labour
	- Wages rises, and increases the cost of production
	- Supply curve is inelastic
- Decreasing cost industry
	- As demand increases, the cost of production decreases
	- Improvement of technology

## Efficiency in Perfectly Competitive Markets

- Quantities of outputs of goods demonstrate productive and allocative efficiency
	- Profit-maximization firms combine with utility-maximizing consumers
- Product efficiency
	- Producing without waste
	- Choice in on the PPF
- In the long run, pice in the market is equal to the min. long-run average cost curve
- Firms produce and sell goods at the lowest possible average cost
- Allocative efficiency
	- The chosen point on the PPF is socially preferred

- A perfectly competitive market will feature both productive and allocative efficiency
- Perfect competition is a hypothetical benchmark
- For market structures such as monopoly, monopolistic competition, and oligopoly
	- Firms will not always produce the min. average cost, or set price equal to MC
- Real world markets include many issues that diverge from the perfect competition
	- Pollution
	- New technology
	- Poverty
	- Government programs
	- Discrimination in labour markets
	- Buyers and sellers with unclear information
