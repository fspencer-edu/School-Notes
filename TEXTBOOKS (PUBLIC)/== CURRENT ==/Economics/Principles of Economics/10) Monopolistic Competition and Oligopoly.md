### Monopolistic Competitors and Entry

- As more firms enter the market, the quantity demanded at a given price will decline
	- Perceived demand curve move to the left
	- MR curve will also move left
	- Change the profit-maximizing quantity, to a lower marginal cost and quantity

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402221608.png" alt="image" width="500">

- The long run equilibrium is in at point Y
	- Perceived demand curve and average cost curve intersect
- When price = AC, economic profits are zero
- The process of new entry will drive down economic profits to zero in the long run

- If a firm is losing money
	- Causes firms to exit, and an increased demand for this particular firm
	- Firms exit up to the point where there are not more losses in this market
	- Demand curve touches the AC, at point Z

- Entry and exit will drive these firms toward a zero economic profit outcome
- Zero economic profit outcome is different than zero economic profit in perfect competition

### Monopolistic Competition and Efficiency

- The long-term result of entry and exit, in perfectly competitive market is that all firms will end up selling at the same price
	- Lowest point on the AC curve
- Monopolistic competition, will result in a point not at the bottom of the AC curve
- Productively inefficient
- Goal is for P > MC
	- Benefits of society of providing addition quantity, as measured as people willing to pay, exceed the marginal costs to society of producing those uses
	- Do not produce more, which results in a loss for society
	- Produce lower quantity of goods and charge a higher price for it, than in a perfectively competitive industry

### The Benefits of Variety and Production Differentiation

- Product differentiation based on variety and innovation
- Critics of market-oriented economies
	- Society does not need differentiation
	- Cost of creating product differentiation, is socially wasteful
- Defenders of a market-oriented economy
	- Consumers benefit when firms seek short-term profits by providing different products

## Oligopoly

- Oligopoly
	- A small number of large firms have all or most of the sales in an industry
- Mutual interdependence where various decisions such as output, price, and advertising depend on other firms' decisions

### Why Do Oligopolies Exist?

- Government grants patent to more than one company
	- Pharmaceutical
	- Military
- A natural monopoly will arise when the quantity demanded in a market is only large enough for a single firm to operate at the min. of the long-run average cost curve
	- Extended quantity results in the market able to withstand only a few oligopoly firms
- Smaller firms have higher average costs, and unable to compete
- Firms may need to reach a certain min. size before becoming a larger scale brand

### Collusion or Competition?

- Collusion
	- Firms act together in a way to reduce output and keep prices hight
- Cartel
	- A group of firms that have a formal agreement to collude to produce the monopoly output and sell at the monopoly price

### The Prisoner's Dilemma

- Game theory
	- A branch of mathematics that analyzes situations in which players mush make decisions and then received payoffs based on what other players decide to do
- Prisoner's dilemma
	- The gains from cooperation are larger than the rewards from pursuing self-interest


### The Oligopoly Version of the Prisoner's Dilemma

- Duopoly
	- Prisoner's dilemma for a two-firm oligopoly

### How to Enforce Cooperation

- Sign a contract
- International agreements to act like a monopoly, hold down output, and keep prices high so that all countries can make high profits
- Oligopolists may choose generate pressure on each firm to stick to its agreed quantity of output
- Kinked demand curve
	- Competing oligopoly firms commit to match price cuts, but not price increases
	- Mange to hold down output, increase price, and share a monopoly level of profits without legally enforceable agreement

### Tradeoffs of Imperfect Competition

- Monopolistic competition is the most common market structure in the US
	- Incentives for innovation
	- Firms seek to earn profit in the short run
	- Entry assures that firms do not earn economic profits in the long run
- Does not produce the lower point on AC curve
- Differentiation leads to excessive social expenses

- Oligopoly is the second most common market structure
	- Take advantage of economies of scale
	- Buffered by significant barriers to entry
	- Earn sustained profits over long periods of time
	- Lack incentives to innovate and provide high-quality service
