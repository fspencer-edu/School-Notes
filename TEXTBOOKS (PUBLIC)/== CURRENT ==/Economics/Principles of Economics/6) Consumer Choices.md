## How Changes in Income and Prices Affect Consumption Choices

- Use budge constraint framework to analyze how quantities demanded change because of price movements

### How Changes in Income Affect Consumer Choices

- Income levels affect consumer choices

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402115611.png" alt="image" width="500">

- If Kimberly's income double, how does this affect the budge constraint
- When income rises, the most common reaction is to purchase more of both goods (N)
- When income falls, the most typical reaction is type purchase less of both goods
	- Normal goods
- A rise in income could cause consumption of one good to increase, while the other declines (P or Q)
- Goods where demand declines as income rises are called inferior goods


### How Price Changes Affect Consumer Choices

- Price increase for baseball bat have no affect on the ability to purchase cameras, but it would reduce the number of bats Sergei could afford to buy
- A price increase for baseball bats, causes the budget constraint to rotate inward, from the vertical axis
- M represents the originally preferred point


<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402115630.png" alt="image" width="500">


- After the price increases
	- Consumers more cameras and fewer bats (H)
	- Consumes more bat, fewer cameras (L)
	- Consumes less of both (J)

- Substitution effect
	- A price changes and consumers have an incentive to consumer less of the good with the higher price, and more of a good with lower price
- Income effect
	- A higher price means that buying power of income has been reduced, which leads of buying of less of good (normal)
- Higher price causes reduced consumption of the good, but can also affect the consumption of other goods

### The Foundations of Demand Curves

- Changes in the price of a good lead the budget constraint to rotate
- A rotation in the budget constraint means that when individuals are seeking their highest utility, the quantity that is demanded of that good will change

### Applications in Government and Business

## Behavioural Economics: An Alternative Framework for Customer Choice

- People sometimes make decisions that seem irrational and not in their best interest
- Traditional economic model
	- Assume rationality
		- People take all available information and make consistent and informed decisions
- Behaviour economics
	- Traditional methods omit people's state of mind
	- Seek to enrich the understanding of decision-making by integrating the insights of psychology into economics
- Tend to focus more on the loss, than the gain
	- Loss aversion
- Loss of $1 is 2.25 times more than $1 gain
- People tend to "overplay" the stock market by reacting more to losses than to gains
- Traditional economics assume that human beings have complete self control
- "Nudges"
	- Establishing ways for people to keep themselves free of temptations
	- Toward rational behaviour
	- Opposed to mandatory regulations from government
	- New employees enrolling in retirement saving plans
- Fungible
	- Dollars
	- Equal value to the individual, regardless of the situation
	- Mental accounting
		- Inconsistent to traditional economist
