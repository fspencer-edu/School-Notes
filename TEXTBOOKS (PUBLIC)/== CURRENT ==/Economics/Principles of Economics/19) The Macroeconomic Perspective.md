### Goals

- Economic growth
	- Determines the standard of living in a country
	- Percentage change in gross domestic product
	- Growth rate of more than 3% is considered good
- Low unemployment
	- Percentage of people in the labour force who do not have a job
	- When people lack jobs, the economy is wasting resource-labour, and results in lower goods and services produced
	- Unemployment rate of 5% or less is considered good
- Low inflation
	- Sustained increased in the overall level of prices
	- Consumer price index
	- Inflation rate of 1-2% is the goal

## Measuring the Size of the Economy: Gross Domestic Product

- Gross domestic product (GDP)
	- Value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a year
- Calculating the production of different goods and services and the price into a dollar value
- US GDP was $20.9 trillion (2020)
- Measure an economy's GDP either by total dollar value of what consumers purchase, or by the total dollar value of what the country produces

### GDP Measured by Components of Demand

- 4 parts of demand
	- Consumer spending (consumption)
	- Business spending (investment)
	- Government spending
	- Spending on net exports

- Business investments make a smaller proportion, and is more volatile

- Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA)
	- Every 5 years the Bureau of the Census carries out a detailed census of businesses through the US
	- Government adjusts figures with foreign trade data to account for exports that are produced in the US and sold abroad as well as for imports

	- Agency for Health Care Research and Quality
	- Department of Agriculture
- BEA produces GDP estimates on a quarterly basis

GDP net export component
- Exports - imports = (X - M)

- Trade balance
	- Gap between exports and imports
- Trade surplus
	- If the country's exports are larger than its imports
- Trade deficit
	- Imports exceed exports

GDP = Consumption + Investment + Government + Trade balance
GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)

### Another Way to Measure GDP - The National Income Approach

- GDP is a measure of what is produced in a nation
- The primary way GDP is estimated is with the Expenditure Approach
- Wages and salaries for labour, interest and dividends for capital, rend for land, profit is income produced in a year can also be used to measure GDP

- National income
	- Total value of a nation's output is equal to the total value of a nation's income

### The Problem of Double Counting

- Double counting
	- Count output more than one as it travels through the production stages
- Final goods and services
	- Change of production that are sold for consumption, investment, government, and trade purposes
- Intermediate goods
	- Goods that go into producing other goods

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260405170942.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Sales of used goods are not included because they were produced in a pervious year
- The underground economy of service paid "under the table" and illegal sales are not tracked and therefore unable to be counted
- Transfer payments, are not included because they do not represent production

### Other Ways to Measure the Economy

- Gross national product (GNP)
	- GDP includes only what country produces within its borders
	- GNP adds what domestic businesses and labour abroad produces, and subtracts any payments that foreign labour and businesses located in the US send home to other countries
- Net national product (NNP)
	- GNP subtracted by the value of how much physical capital is worn out, reduced in value because of agin, over the course of a year
- Deprecation
	- Capital that ages and loses value
- National income
	- Includes all income to businesses and individuals, and personal income, which includes only income to people
- Gross national income (GNI)
	- Value of all goods and services produced by people from a country
	- Wealth of a nation because it focuses on income, not output
	- Used by World Bank to classify national economic status

## Adjusting Nominal Values to Real Values

- Nominal value
	- The economic statistic that is measured is that statistic in terms of actual prices that exist at the time
- Real value
	- Refers to the same statistic after is has been adjusted for inflation

## Tracking Real GDP over Time

- Governments report GDP growth at an annualized rate

- Recession
	- Significant decline in real GDP
- Depression
	- Lengthly and deep recession
- Peak
	- Highest point of the economy, before the rescission begins
- Trough
	- Lowest point before a recovery beings
- Business cycle
	- Economy's movement from peak to trough, and vice versa
- National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER)
	- Tracks business cycles in the US

## How Well GDP Measures the Well Being of Society

- Standard of living
	- Includes all elements that affect people's well bring
	- Whether they are bought and sold in the market or not

### Limitations of GDP as a Measure of the Standard of Living

- GDP measure economic activity, not all activity
- Does not account unpaid work or leisure time
- Does not include levels of environmental cleanliness, health, and learning
	- Air and water quality
	- Life expectancy or infant mortality
	- Does not address population success in reading, writing, or mathematics
- Does not cover production that is not exchanged in the market
	- Local, smaller business
- Not a clear indicator of inequality or technological growth
- Does not take into account extreme cases
	- Rebuilding construction activity during an a weather disaster
	- Crime


### Does a Raise in GDP Overstate or Understate the Rise in the Standard of Living?

- The rise in GDP understates the actual rise in the standard of living

### GDP Is Rough, but Useful

- A high level of GDP should not by the only goal of macroeconomic policy, or government policy
