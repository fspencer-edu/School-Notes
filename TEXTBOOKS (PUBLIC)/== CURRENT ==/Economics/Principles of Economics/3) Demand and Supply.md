## Demand, Supply, and Equilibrium in Markets for Goods and Services

### Demands for Goods and Services

- Demand
	- Amount of some good or service consumers are willing and able to purchase at each price
	- Based on needs and wants
- Price
	- Unit of a specific good or service that a buyers pays
- Quantity demanded
	- Total number of units that consumers purchase
- Law of demand
	- Inverse relationship between price and quantity demand
	- Assumes all other variables are held constant
- Demand schedule
	- Table showing the quantity of a good consumers are willing and able to purchase at various price levels
- Demand curve
	- Relationship between price and quantity demanded on a graph

### Supply of Goods and Services

- Supply
	- Amount of some good or service a producer is willing to supply at each price
- Quantity supplied
	- Specific amount of good producers are willing and able to sell at a particular market price
- Law of supply
	- Positive relationship between price and quantity
	- A higher price leads to a high quantity supplied
	- Lowed price leads to a lower quantity supplied
- Supply schedule
	- Shows the quantity supplied at a range of different prices
- Supply curve
	- Relationship between price, and quantity

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260401232107.png" alt="image" width="500">

### Equilibrium - Where Demand and Supply Intersect

- Demand and supply determine the price and the quantity that will be bought and sold in a market

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260401232151.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Demand curve (D) and supply curve (S) intersect at the equilibrium point E (600, $1.40)
- Equilibrium
	- Point where the S and D curves cross
- Equilibrium price
	- Only price where the plans of consumers and plans of producers agree
- Equilibrium quantity
	- Amount of goods supplied and demanded in a market when the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied ($Q_d = Q_s$)

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260401232543.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Above equilibrium price
	- Quantity demand drops
	- Excess supply or surplus
- Below equilibrium price
	- Demand increases
	- Excess demand or shortage


## Shifts in Demand and Supply for Goods and Services

### What Factors Affect Demand?

- Tastes and preferences
	- Willingness to purchase a item at a given price

### The Ceteris Paribus Assumption

- Ceteris paribus
	- "other things being equal"
	- A demand curve or s supply curve is a relationship between only two variables
	- Other variables are constant

### How Does Income Affect Demand?

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260401233114.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Higher income causes greater demand at every price
- Normal good
	- A product whose demand rises when income rises
- Inferior good
	- A product whose demand falls when income rises

### Other Factors that Shift Demand Curves

**Changes Tastes of Preferences**

- Movement in tastes for produce

**Changes in the Composition of the Population**

- Proportion of population
	- Increasing aging population

**Changes in the Prices of Related Goods**

- Substitute
	- A good or service that we can use in place of another
- A lower price for a substitute decreases demand for the other product
- Complements
	- Consumption of one good tends to enhance consumption of the other

**Changes in Expectations about Future Prices or Other Factors that Affect Demand**
- Shift in demand
	- Change in economic factor causes a different quantity to be demanded at very price

#### Shift in Demand

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260401233915.png" alt="image" width="500">

- When a demand curve shifts, it will intersect with a given supply curve at a different equilibrium price and quantity

### How Production Costs Affect Supply

- Shift in supply
	- A change in the quantity supplied at every price
- When cost of production falls, a firm will tend to supply a lager quantity at any given price for its outputs

### Other Factors That Affect Supply

- Inputs or factors of production
	- Goods and services a firm produces
- Changes in weather or other natural conditions
- New technologies for production
- Government policies

- Government subsidy
	- Opposite of a tax
	- Occurs when the government pays a firm directly or reduces the firm's taxes if the firm carries out certain actions

## Changes in Equilibrium Prices and Quantity: The 4 Step Process

- Draw a demand and supply model
	- Law of demand
		- Demand curve is negative
	- Law of supply
		- Supply curve is positive
	- Shift variables for demand
	- Shift variables for supply
	- Find the initial equilibrium values for price and quantity
- Find economic change
- The effect on demand of supply causes the curve to shift to the right or left
- Identify the new equilibrium

## Prices Ceilings and Price Floors

- A small number of fundamental principles explain how economic agents respond in different situations
	- Laws of demand and supply
- Governments can pass laws affect market outcomes
- No law can negate these economic principles

### Price Ceilings

- Price controls
	- Laws that governments enact to regulate prices
		- Price ceiling
			- Keeps a price from rising above a certain level
		- Price floor
			- Keeps a price from falling below a given level

- Price ceiling
	- A legal maximum price that one pays for some good or service
	- Rent control


<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402084323.png" alt="image" width="500">

- When market price is not allowed to rise to the equilibrium level
	- Quantity demanded exceed quantity suppled, causes a shortage

### Price Floors

- Lowest price that one can legally pay for some good or service
	- Minimum wage
- The most common way price supports work is that the government enters the market and buts the product, adding to demand to keep prices higher than they otherwise would be
	- Common Agricultural Policy reform
- A quantity supplied in excess of the quantity demanded causes a surplus

## Demand, Supply, and Efficiency

- Efficiency
	- When it is impossible to improve the situation of one party without imposing a cost on another
- Inefficient
	- Possible to benefit at least on party without imposing costs on others

- Economy is getting as much benefit as possible from its scarce resources and all the possible gains from trade have been achieved
- Optimal amount of each good and service is produced and consumed

### Consumer Surplus, Producer Surplus, Social Surplus

- Consumer surplus
	- Amount that individuals would have been willing to pay, minus the amount actually paid

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402085152.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Producer surplus
	- Price the producer actually received minus the price the producer would have been wiling to accept
- Social surplus
	- Sum of consumer surplus and producer surplus
		- Also called economic surplus or total surplus
	- $F + G$ area
- Social surplus is larger at equilibrium quantity and price, that is would be at any other quantity
- Economic efficiency of the market equilibrium
- At efficient level of output, it is impossible to produce greater consumer surplus without reducing producer surplus, and vice versa

### Inefficiency of Price Floors and Price Ceilings

- The imposition of a price floor or a price ceiling will prevent a market from adjusting to its equilibrium price and quantity
	- Creating an inefficient outcome
- Price floors and ceilings will also transfer some consumer surplus to producers, or vice versa

- Deadweight loss
	- The loss in social surplus that occurs when the economy produces at an inefficient quantity
	- $U+W$ area
- Both consumer and producer surplus could be higher
	- Price control blocks some suppliers and demanders from transactions
- Price ceiling can transfer producer surplus to consumers
	- The new consumer surplus is T + V, while the new producer surplus is X
	- Price ceiling transfers the area of surplus (V), producers -> consumers
	- Gain to customers is less than the loss to producers (deadweight loss)

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402090144.png" alt="image" width="500">


- Price ceilings
	- Transfers some producer surplus to consumers
- Price floor
	- Transfers consumer surplus to producers
- Both price floors and price ceilings block some transactions that buyers and sellers would have been wiling to make, and create deadweight loss

### Demand and Supply as a Social Adjustment Mechanism

- Price are not set only by demand or supply, but the interaction between the two
- Adjustments of equilibrium price and quantity in a market-oriented economy often occur without much government direction or oversight
