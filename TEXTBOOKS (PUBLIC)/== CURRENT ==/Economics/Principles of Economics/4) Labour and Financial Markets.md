
- Labour markets are markets for employees or jobs
- Financial service markets are markets for saving or borrowing

- In labour market
	- Employees are the suppliers of labour
	- Firms are the demanders

## Demand and Supply at Work in Labour Markets

- The law of demand applies in labour markets
	- Higher salary or wage
		- Decrease in the quantity of labour demanded
	- Lower salary or wage
		- Increase in the quantity of labour demanded
- A higher price for labour leads to a higher quantity of labour supplied, and vice versa

### Equilibrium in the Labour Market

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402091216.png" alt="image" width="500">

- The measure of labour is by the number of workers
	- Another common way to measure the quantity of labour is by the number of hours worked
- Benefits are a significant part of labour compensation
- As the salary for nurses rises, the quantity demanded will fall
- As the salary for nurses rises, the quantity supplied will rise
- At equilibrium
	- The quantity supplied and demanded are equal
- When not at the equilibrium
	- Economic incentives tend to move salaries toward the equilibrium
- Excess supply in the labour market
	- Employers will have an incentive to offer lower wages that they otherwise would have
- Excess demand or shortage
	- Employers will offer higher pay to attract employees

### Shifts in Labour Demand

- A change in the wage will results in a change in the quantity demanded of labour
- As wage rate increases, employees will want to hire less employees
- Quantity of labour demanded will decrease, and there will be movement upward along the demand curve

- As wages decrease, employers will higher large number of workers
- Quantity of labour demanded will increase, resulting in a downward movement along the demand curve

- Demand for labour is based on the demand for the good or service that is produced
- The demand for labour is called a "derived demand"
	- Demand for chefs is dependent on the demand for restaurants

**Factors that can shift demand**
- Demand for output
- Education and training
- Technology
- Number of companies
- Government regulations
- Price and availability of other inputs

### Shifts in Labour Supply

- The supply of labour is a upward curve
	- The higher the price, the greater the quantity supplied
	- The lower the price, the less quantity supplied

**Factors that can shift supply**
- Number of workers
- Required education
- Government Policies

- A change in salary will lead to a movement along labour demand or labour supply curves, but is will not shift those curves

### Price Floors in the Labour Market - Living Wages and Minimum Wages

- Price ceilings are rare in labour markets, because that prevents people from earning income
	- Exception
		- Boards of trustees
		- Stockholders
- Minimum wage
	- A price floor that makes it illegal for employers to pay employees less than a certain hourly rate
- Living wage
	- The minimum wage is too low to ensure a reasonable standard living

### The Minimum Wage as an Example of a Price Floor

- The US minimum wage is a price floor that is set either very close to the equilibrium wage or even slightly below it
- Walter Williams and Thomas Sowell
	- Argue that minimum wages increase discrimination and limit economic mobility
	- Higher minimum wages would increase employment barriers for lower-skilled workers, reducing the opportunity for them to learn on the job and gain experience
- Price floor minimum wage is non-binding
	- Set below equilibrium price
	- Price floor is not determining the market outcome
- Binding
	- Government imposed price set above the market equilibrium
	- Causing a surplus

- Law of demand
	- A higher required wage will reduce the amount of low-skill employment either in terms of employees or in terms of work hours

## Demand and Supply in Financial Markets

- Financial capital supply (savings)
- Financial capital demand (borrowings)

### Who Demands and Who Supplies in Financial Markets?

- In a market
	- The price is what suppliers receive and demanders pay
- In financial markets
	- Those who supply financial capital, expect to received a rate of return
	- Demand financial capital pay a rate of return

- Interest rate
	- The cost of borrowing money or the reward of saving it
	- Usually expressed as an annual percentage of the principal amount

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402094020.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Law of demand
	- A higher rate of return will decrease the quantity demanded
	- At higher interest rate rises, consumers will reduce the quantity that they borrow
- Law of supply
	- A higher price increases the quantity supplied
	- As the interest rate paid on credit card borrowing rises, more firms will be eager to issue credit cards

### Equilibrium in Financial Markets

- The equilibrium occurs at an interest rate of 15%
- If the interest rate is above the equilibrium
	- There is an excess supply of financial capital
	- Firms are more eager to supply loans, but less people are will wiling to borrow
- Interest rate below equilibrium
	- Excess demand or shortage of fund occurs in the market
	- Quantity of borrowers demand, but the quantity of credit firms willing to supply is less


### Shift in Demand and Supply in Financial Markets

- Suppliers of financial capital
	- How much to save
	- How to divide the savings among different forms of investment

- Intertemporal decision making
	- Participants in the financial markets must decide when they prefer to consume goods
- Social Security
	- The program that workers pay into in order to qualify for government checks after retirement
	- Tended to reduce the quantity of financial capital that workers save (shift left)
- When consumers and businesses have greater confidence that they will be able to replay in the future, the quantity demanded of financial capital at any given interest rate will shift to the right

- Suppliers of financial capital will have to consider the rates of return and the risks involved

### Price Ceilings in Financial Markets - Usury Laws

- Political pressures sometimes arise for setting limits on the interest rates or fees from credit card companies
- Firms that issue credit cards, respond that the higher interest rates are necessary to cover the losses created by those who borrow on their credit and do not replay
- The demand and supply model predicts
	- At lower price ceiling interest rate, the quantity demanded of credit card debt will increase from its original level
	- Quantity supplied of credit card debt will decrease
	- Quantity demanded will exceed quantity supplied
	- Credit shortage

- Usury laws
	- Impose an upper limit on the interest rate that lenders can change
	- A price ceiling that is set at a high level is nonbinding
		- No practical effect unless the equilibrium price soars high enough to exceed the price ceiling

## The Market System as an Efficient Mechanism for Information

- Prices serve as a remarkable social mechanism for collecting, combining, and transmitting information that is relevant to the market
- In a market-oriented economy, no government agency or guidance oversees the set of responses and interconnections that results from a change in price
	- Each consumer reacts according to that person's preferences and budget set

**Demand and Supply models**

- Demand and supply model can explain the existing levels of prices, wages, and rates of return
- Also used to explain how economic events will cause changes

- An increase in the price of some product signals consumers that there is a shortage
- Price changes provide useful information to producers
- A change in any single market is transmitted through multiple interconnections to other markets
- Price controls are government laws that serve to regulate prices
- Price controls do nothing to affect the underlying forces of demand and supply
- Immobilizing the price messenger through price controls will deprive people in the economy of critical information
	- Causes unpredictable behaviour
