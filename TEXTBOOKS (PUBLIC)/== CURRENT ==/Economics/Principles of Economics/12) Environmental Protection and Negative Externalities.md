## The Economic of Pollution

### Externalities

- Externality
	- The effect of a market exchange on a third party who is outside to the exchange
		- Negative externality
		- Positive externality
- Spillovers
	- Externalities that occur in market transactions affect other parties beyond those involved
### Pollution as a Negative Externality

- Social costs
	- The total cost of an economic activity to society
	- Sum of private costs and external costs

- Additional external costs
	- Move the supply curve up, increasing the demand price
- Market failture
	- Where externalities represent a case where markets no longer consider all social costs, but only some of them
	- Private market fails to achieve efficient output
- Pollution
	- Social costs of production exceed social benefits to consumers, and the market produces too much of the product

## Command-and-Control Regulation

- Command-and-control regulation
	- Requires that firms increase their costs by installing anti-pollution equipment
- Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)
- Clean Air Act
- Clean Water Act
- Offer not incentive to improve the quality of the environment beyond the standard set by a particular law
- Inflexible
	- Requires the same standard for all polluters, and same pollution control technology
- Subject to political process, loopholes, and exceptions


## Market Oriented Environmental Tools

- Market oriented environmental policies create incentives to allow firms some flexibility in reducing pollution
	- Pollution charges
	- Marketable permits
	- Better-defined property rights

### Pollution Charges

- Pollution charge
	- A tax imposed on the quantity of pollution that a firm emits
	- Profit-maximizing firms an incentive to determine ways to reduce its emissions

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260404123929.png" alt="image" width="500">

- A firm that has a pollution tax will have an incentive to find the least expensive technologies for reducing pollution
	- Gas tax
	- Refundable charge for returning recyclable cans and bottles

### Marketable Permits

- Marketable permit program (cap-and-trade)
	- Determining the overall quantity of pollution it will allow as it tries to meet national pollution standards
	- Divided a number of permits allowing only this quantity of pollution among the firms that emit that pollutant
	- Sell or provide permits to pollute free to firms
	- Allows firms with low abatement costs to sell excess permits to those with higher costs
- Permits are shrinkable
	- The amount of pollution allowed by a given permit declined with time

### Better-Defined Property Rights

- Property rights
	- The legal rights of ownership on which others are not allowed to infringe without paying compensation
	- Endangered species
		- "Shoot, shovel, and shut up"
	- A more productive policy would consider how to provide private landowners with an incentive to protect the endangered species on their and

### Applying Market-Oriented Environmental Tools

- Marketable permits work best when a few dozen or hundred parties are interested in trading
	- Oil refineries

## The Benefits and Costs of US Environmental Laws

### Benefits and Costs of Clean Air and Clean Water

- Benefits of a cleaner environment
	- People stay healthier and live longer
	- Certain industries that rely on clean air and water, benefit
	- Property values may be higher
	- People can enjoy a cleaner environment

### Ecotourism - Making Environmentalism Pay

- Ecotourism need careful management
	- Governments have enacted policies where they share revenues from ecotourism with local communities

### Marginal Benefits and Marginal Costs

- Qa
	- When the quantity of environmental protection is low so that pollution is extensive
	- There are numerous relatively cheap and easy ways to reduce pollution
- Qb
	- The gap between marginal benefits and marginal costs narrow
- Qc
	- Marginal costs will exceed the marginal benefits

- US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)
	- Estimate the value of saving lives by reducing pollution against the additional costs
- Center for Environmental Economics (NCEE) values a statistical human life at $7.4 M (2006)
- Government regulators use estiminates when deciding what proposed regulations are reasonable
- Department of Transportation
	- Approve rules only where the estimated cost per life saved is $3 M or less
- When a regulation costs $50 M, it diverts enough spending in the rest of the economy from health care and safety expenditures that is costs a life
