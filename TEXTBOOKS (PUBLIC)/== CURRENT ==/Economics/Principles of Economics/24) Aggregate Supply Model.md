## Macroeconomic Perspectives on Demand and Supply

- 2 groups of macroeconomists
	- Those who argue that supply is the most important determinant of the size of the macroeconomy
	- Those who argue that demand the the most important

### Say's Law and the Macroeconomics of Supply

- Say's law
	- Supply creates its own demand
- Neoclassical economics
	- Important of supply for determining the size of the macroeconomy
	- Jean-Baptiste, Adam Smith
- Recession occurs when the economy as a whole is shrinking, and business failures outnumber the remaining success stories
- Over long periods of time, as the productive power of an economy to supply goods and services increases, total demand in the economy grows at roughly the same pace
- Shorter periods of time, recessions can occur for a lack of demand for their product

### Keynes' Law and the Macroeconomics of Demand

- Keynes' law
	- Demand creates its own supply
- During the Depression, the economy's capacity to supply goods and services had not changed much as US unemployment rates increased
- Recessions were not caused by a group in the ability of the economy to supply goods as measured by labour, physical capital, or technology
- Economy produced less that its full potential because of the lack of demand
- Applies to short run, when many firms experience either a drop in demand for their output during a recession or having trouble meeting demands during a economic boom

### Combining Supply and Demand in Macroeconomics

- The tradeoffs and connections between the 3 goals of macroeconomics may be different in the shirt run and long run
	- Economic growth (rising GDP)
	- Full employment
	- Price stability (low inflation)

- Aggregate demand/aggregate supply model
	- Determines total supply or total demand for the economy
	- How total demand and total supply interact at the macroeconomic levels

### The Aggregate Supply Curve and Potential GDP

- Firms make decisions about that what quantity to supply based on the profits they expect to earn

- Aggregate supply (AS)
	- Total quantity of output firms will produce and sell
- Aggregate supply (AS) curve
	- Total quantity of output that firms will produce and sell at each price lvel

- As the price level rises, real GDP rises as well

- Potential GDP
	- Amount of real GDP an economy can produce by fully employing its existing levels of labour, physical, capital, and technology
	- At low levels of output, levels of unemployment are high, and factories are running only part-time
	- A relatively small increase in the prices of the outputs that business sell can encourage a considerable increase in the quantity of aggregate supply from a work force to enter into production
	- A higher price level for output continuous to encourage a greater quantity of output, but at a increasing aggregate supply

- Firms are able to produce past potential labour through hyper-intense production
	- Double-overtime
	- 24 hours a day
	- 7 says a week

- Full employment GDP
	- When machines and factories are running at capacity and unemployment rate is relatively low
	- At the natural rate of unemployment

### The Aggregate Demand Curve

- Aggregate demand (AD)
	- Amount of total spending on domestic gods and services in an economy
	- Consumption, investment, government spending, and net exports
- Aggregate demand (AD) curve
	- Total spending on domestic goods and services at each price level

- Higher price level for final outputs reduces aggregate demand

- If equilibrium occurs in the flat range of AS, then the economy is not close to potential GDP and will experience unemployment, but stable price levels
- If equilibrium occurs in the steep range of AS, then the economy is close or at potential GDP and will be experiencing rising price levels or inflationary pressures, but will have a low unemployment rate


### Equilibrium in the Aggregate Demand/Aggregate Supply Model

- At a low price level for output, firms will have little incentive to produce, although consumer will be willing to buy more
- When price level rises, aggregate supply rises and demand falls, where equilibrium is not reached

### Defining SRAS and LRAS

- Short run aggregate supply (SRAS) curve
	- An upward-sloping line showing a positive relationship between the price level and total output that firms are willing to produce
- Long run aggregate supply (LRAS) curve
	- A vertical line representing an economy's max potential output at full employment

## Shifts in Aggregate Supply

### How Productivity Growth Shifts the AS Curve

- In the long run, the most important factor of the AS curve is productivity growth
- Real growth in GDP per capital in the US has averaged 2-3% per year
	- Productivity has been faster

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260406111140.png" alt="image" width="500">

- A shirt in the SRAS curve to the right results in a greater real GDP and downward pressure on the price level, if aggregate demand remains unchanged
- If the shirt results from gains in productivity, the effect will be relatively small

### How Changes in Input Prices Shift the AS Curve

- Higher prices for inputs are widely used across the entire economy
- Stagflation
	- Shifting SRAS left leading to a stagnant economy with high unemployment and inflation
	- A decline in price, will move SRAS to the right, providing an incentive for more to be produced at every given price level for outputs

### Other Supply Shocks

- The AS curve can also shift due to shocks to inputs goods or labour
	- Unexpected crop disease
	- Overseas war
