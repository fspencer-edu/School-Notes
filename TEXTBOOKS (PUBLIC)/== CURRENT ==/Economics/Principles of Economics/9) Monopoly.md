
- Monopoly

	- No competition

## How Monopolies Form: Barriers to Entry

- Barriers to entry
	- Legal, technological, or market forces that discourage or prevent potential competitors from entering a market

**5 Types of Monopoly**
1) Natural
2) Legal
3) Geographic
4) Technological
5) Government

### Natural Monopoly

- Economies of scale can combine with the size of the market to limit competition

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402211706.png" alt="image" width="500">

Natural monopoly
- Economies of scale are large relative to the quantity demanded in the market
- Marginal cost of adding an additional customer is low, once the fixed costs of the overall system are in place
	- Water company
- Can arise when smaller local markets for products are difficult to transport
	- Cement production

### Control of a Physical Resource

- A company has control of a scarce physical resource
	- ALCOA (Aluminum Company of America)
	- Control most of the supply of bauxite
- Global diamond production is controlled by DeBeers

### Legal Monopoly

- Government creates barriers to entry by prohibiting or limiting competition
	- Postal service

### Promoting Innovation

- Constitution of the US
	- Congress shall have Power to Promote the Progress of Science and Useful Arts
- Patent
	- Gives the inventor the exclusive legal right to make, use, or sell the invention for a limited time
- Trademark
	- An identifying symbol or name for a particular good
- Copyright
	- A form of protection provided by the laws of the US for original work of authorship
- Trade secretes
	- Competing firms are not allowed to steal a firms secretes
- Intellectual property
	- Implies ownership over an idea, concept, or image, not a physical piece of property
	- World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO)
- Deregulation
	- Eliminated or reduced government restrictions on firms

### Intimidating Potential Competition

- Predatory pricing
	- A firm uses the threat of sharp price cuts to discourage competition
	- Violation of US antitrust law, difficult to prove

## How a Profit-Maximizing Monopoly Chooses Output and Price

- Profits for the monopolist, will be equal to total revenues minus total costs
	- Total cost, fixed, cost, variables cost, marginal cost, average cost, and average variable cost
- No competition
	- The price will be decided differentnly

### Demand Curves Perceived by the Perfectly Competitive Firm and by a Monopoly

- A perfect competitive firm acts as a price taker

- If a firm produces a product without close substitutes, then it is considered a monopoly producer
- If buyers have a range of options, then the firm is not a monopoly

	- A monopoly perceived demand for its product in a market where the monopoly is the only producer

### Marginal Revenue and Marginal Cost for a Monopolist

- A monopolist does not have enough information to analyze its entire total revenues or total costs curves
- Information about changing output by small or moderate amounts from marginal revenues and marginal costs, can help guide decisions
- Marginal revenue is zero at quantity 7
- Marginal revenue become negative at quantities higher than 7
- As the quantity sold becomes higher, as some point the group in price is proportionally more than the increase in the greater quantity of sales

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402213832.png" alt="image" width="500">

- The profit maximizing price and quantity is where the marginal revenue and marginal costs of producing an extra unit intersect
- The monopoly could seek out the profit maximizing level of output by increasing quantity by a small amount, and calculating marginal revenue and cost
- Follow this process until MR = MC

### The Inefficiency of Monopoly

- Allocative efficiency
	- Efficiency at the social or societal level
	- Producing the optimal quantity of some output
	- Marginal benefits to society or one or more unit equals the marginal costs
- Firms strive for new inventions and new intellectual property
- Monopolies can be created from competitive pressures
- Monopolies can rely on old products, and are less incentivized to innovate and compete
