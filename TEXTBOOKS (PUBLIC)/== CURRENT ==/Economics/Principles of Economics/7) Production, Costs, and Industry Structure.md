## Explicit and Implicit Costs, and Accounting and Economic Profit

Profit = Total revenue - total cost
Total revenue = price x quantity

- Total revenue
	- The income the firm generates from selling its products
- Total cost 
	- What the firm pays for producing and selling it products

- 2 Types of costs
	- Explicit
	- Implicit

- Explicit costs
	- Out-of-packet costs
	- Employee salaries
	- Rend
- Implicit costs
	- Opportunity costs using resources that the firm already owns
	- Owner no earning a formal salary
	- Company working out of a house

- Accounting profit
	- Cash concept
	- Total revenue - explicit costs
	- Determined taxes
- Economic profit
	- Total revenue - total cost
	- Includes explicit and implicit counts


#### Calculating Implicit Costs

Expected earnings = $200k/year
Cost = $50k/year
Law clerk = $35k/year

Total cost = 50k + 35k = $85k
Accounting profit = 200k - 85k = $115k

- The calculation only considers the explicit costs
- To open the practice, she losses her salary of 125k

Economic profit = total revenues - explicit costs - implicit costs
$= 200k - 85k - 125k = -10k$

## Production in the Short Run

- Production is the process a firm uses to transform inputs into outputs

**Categories of Production**
- Natural resources (land and raw materials)
- Labour
- Capital
- Technology
- Entrepreneurship


- The cost of production depends on the inputs costs
- Production function
	- Relationship between inputs and outputs

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402140228.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Production functions are specific to the product
- Inputs
	- Fixed
	- Variable

- Fixed inputs
	- Cannot be easily increased or decreased in a short period of time
	- Building
	- Define the firm's max output capacity
- Variable inputs
	- Can easily be increased or decreased in a short period of time
	- Ingredients

Production Function => $Q = f[L, K]$

L = variable inputs
K = fixed inputs

- Short run
	- Period of time during which at least some factors of production are fixed
	- Restaurant lease
- Long run
	- All factors are variable

Capital is fixed in the short run
$Q = f[L, \bar{K}]$ or $Q = f[L]$

- Since capital is fixed, the amount of output depends only on labour of employees

- Output (Q) = Total Product (TP)
	- Amount of output produced with a given amount of labour and a fixed amount of capital
- Marginal product
	- Additional output of one more worker

$MP = \Delta TP/\Delta L$

- Law of Diminishing Marginal Product
	- Adding more of one variable input to fixed input will eventually yield in lower incremental output per unitt

### Average and Marginal Costs

- The cost of producing a firm's output depends on how much labour and physical capital the firm uses
- 2 ways to measure per unit costs
	- Average cost
	- Marginal cost

- Average cost
	- Total cost divided by quantity of output produced
	- $AC = TC/Q$
- Marginal cost
	- Change in total cost divided by the change in output
	- $MC = \Delta TC/\Delta Q$

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402141912.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Marginal cost of the first unit of output is always the same as total cost

### Fixed and Variable Costs

- Decompose costs into fixed and variable costs
- Fixed costs
	- Capital
	- Expenditures that do not change regardless of the level of production
	- Amount of fixes costs varies to business types
		- Manufacturing requires an expensive factor
		- A remote online business has little fixed costs
- Variable costs
	- Only way to increase or decrease output is by increasing or decreasing the variable inputs
	- Labour
	- Raw materials
- Total costs are the sum of fixed and variables costs

### Average Total Cost, Average Variable Cost, Marginal Cost

- The breakdown of total costs into fixed and variable costs can provide a basis for other insights
	- Average total costs
	- Average variable costs
	- Marginal costs
- Measured costs on per/unit rather than total

- Average total cost
	- Also called average cost
	- Total cost / quantity of output
	- U shaped
	- Start high, decreases, then increases
	- Fixed costs are spread over an increasing quantity of output
	- As the output expands, the average cost rises
- Average variable cost
	- Variable cost / quantity of output
	- U-shaped
	- As output grows, fixed costs become less important 
- Marginal cost
	- Additional cost of producing one more unit of output
	- Change in total cost and dividing it by the change in quantity
	- Upward-sloping
	- Small increase in marginal return as a dip in the marginal cost before rising
- Point where marginal and average costs meet

## Costs in the Long Run

- The long run is the period of time when all costs are variable
- No costs are fixed
- Firms with compare alternative production technologies/processes

### Choice of Production Technology

- A firm can perform many tasks with a range of combinations of labour and physical capital
- Firms often face a choice between buying many small machines, or a large more expensive machine with less workers
- Physical capital and labour can substitute for each other
- A profit-seeking firm will choose the production technology that is least expensive, given the prices of labour and machines

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402144330.png" alt="image" width="500">

- As input become more expensive, firms will attempt to convert on using that input, and shift to less expensive methods
- Demand curve of labour slopes down
- Alternatively, an employer is likely to use production technologies with more workers and less machinery when producing in a lower-wage country is cheaper

### Economies of Scale

- After determining the least costly production technology, a firm also considers the optimal scale of production
- As the quantity increases, the cost per unit decreases

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402144648.png" alt="image" width="500">

- The economies of scale curve is a long-run average cost curve
	- Allows all factors of production to change
- Short-run average cost curves, only allows variable costs to change and are U-shaped

### Shapes of Long-Run Average Cost Curves

- Short runs are limited to operating on a single average cost curve
- Long run firms can choose to operate on any average cost curve

- Long-run average cost (LRAC) curve
	- Based on a group of short-run average cost (SRAC) curve

- Each SRAC represents one specific level of fixed cost
- LRAC is the least expensive average cost curve for any level of output

### The Size and Number of Firms in an Industry


<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260402144850.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Grouping economic activity is more productive in many cases than spreading it out
	- Large cities are sometimes called "agglomeration economies"
- At some point, agglomeration economies turn into diseconomies
	- Traffic congestion
	- High density
	- Increased housing prices
	- Increased population
	- Overcrowded
	- Increased crime

- Flat section in the long-run average cost curve
	- A single plant has the same average costs as a plant producing 4x the capacity
	- One firm owns a single plant, while another firms own 4 separate plant that produce the same
- Oligopoly
	- A market structure dominated by a small number of large firms
	- Control market prices
- Monopoly
	- A structure where a single seller dominates the entire industry, offering a unique product or service with no close substitutes

- If the LRAC curve has a single point at the bottom, the firm in the market will be the same size
- If the LRAC curve is flat, then the firms in the market may be variety of different sizes

- The relationship between the quantity at the min. of the LRAC and the quantity of demanded in the market at that price will predict how much competition exists
- If quantity demanded in market exceed the min. of LRAC
	- Firms will compete
- If quantity demanded in market is only slightly higher than the quantity at the min.
	- Few firms will compete
- If quantity demanded is less
	- A single producer monopoly is a likely outcome
