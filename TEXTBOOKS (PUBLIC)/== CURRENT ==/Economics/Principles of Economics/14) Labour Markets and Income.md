## The Theory of Labour Markets

- Labour market
	- Used for all different markets for labour
	- There is a different market for every type of labour
	- Labour differs by type of work, skill level, and location
	- Labour market has demand and supply
- First rule of labour markets
	- If a firm wants to maximize profits, it will never pay more for a worker than the value of their marginal productivity to the firm
- Marginal product
	- Additional output a firm can product by adding one more worker to the production process
	- Also defined as the additional output the firm produced by adding one more worker hour to the production process

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260404145026.png" alt="image" width="500">

- The value of each worker's output will be the market price of the product
	- Assumed perfectly competitive market

Demand for labour = $MP_L \times P$ = value of marginal product of labour

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260404145240.png" alt="image" width="500">

- If $VMP_L$ > Wage => hire more workers
- If $VMP_L$ < Wage => stop hiring

- Add the amount of workers increase, the $MP_L$ declines
- Hiring more workers increases production, but is less efficient over time (diminishing returns)

### Demand for Labour in Perfectly Competitive Output Markets

- Perfectly competitive labour market
	- Firms can hire all the labour at the going market wage
- Horizontal supply curve for labour

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260404145621.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Profit maximizing firms hire workers up to the point where, $W_{market} = VMP_L$

- Derived demand
	- Where the demand for a good, service, of factor of production is caused by the demand for a different, often final, consumer product
- An increase in demand for products, increases the price, which increases the demand for labour


### Demand for Labour in Imperfectly Competitive Output Markets

- If a firm does not sell its output in a perfectly competitive industry, they face a downward sloping demand curve for output
- To sell additional output the firm must lower its price
- The value of a worker's marginal product is the marginal revenue, not the price

Demand for labour = $MP_L \times MR$ = Marginal revenue product


<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260404150127.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Given the market wage, profit-maximizing firms will hire workers up to the point where the market wage = marginal revenue product


<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260404150207.png" alt="image" width="500">

- $L_2$
	- For firms with market power in their output market
	- The going market wage equals the firm's marginal revenue product
- $L_1$
	- Demand for labour for a firm which has market power in its output market is less than the demand for labour
	- Employment will be lower in an imperfectly competitive industry, than a perfectly competitive industry


### What Determines the Going Market Wage Rate?

- Labour market has demand and supply curve like other markets
- The demand for labour curve is a downward sloping function of the wage rate
- The supply of labour curve is an upward sloping function of the wage rate
- The supply of labour is the horizontal summations of all the individuals supplied of labour

- Monopsony
	- A market structure with only one buyer or a dominant buyer controlling many sellers
	- Dictate the price
- Marginal cost of labour
	- The cost to a firm of hiring one more worker

- Marginal cost increases faster than the wage rate
- To hire one more worker requires paying a higher wage rate, not just for that worker, but for all previous hires

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260404151751.png" alt="image" width="500">

- A monopsony will hire workers up to the point $L_m$, where its demand for labour = marginal cost of additional labour
- To maximize profits, it will hire labour up to the point $L_m$ where $D_L = VMP (MRP) = MC_L$
- The supply curve for labour shows the wage the firm will have to pay to attract $L_m$ workers
- A competitive market operates where $D_L = S_L$
- Under monopsony employers hire fewer workers and pay a lower wage

## Market Power on the Supply Side of Labour Markets: Unions

- Labour union
	- An organization of workers that negotiates with employees over wages and working conditions
	- Balance power between employers and workers
	- Requires employers to deal with workers collectively
- Collective bargaining
	- Negotiations between unions and firms

### Facts about Union Membership and Pay

- 10.3% of all US workers belong to unions
- Union members earn about 20% more than non-union workers
- Unions have been declining for 50 yeas

### Higher Wages for Union Workers

- If no labour union exists, then equilibrium (E) in the labour market would occur at the intersection of the demand and supply for labour
- Labour unions are able to increase the equilibrium wage

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260404152900.png" alt="image" width="500">

- At the higher union wage, the firms will hire less labour than they would have hired in equilibrium
- An excess supply of workers want union jobs
- A response could be that a firm decides to purchase inputs from non-union producers, than with their own paid unionized workers
- Move to a state or country where unions are less powerful
- Union workers might have higher productivity than non-union workers
	- Higher wage may elicit higher productivity
	- Union workers tend to stay longer at a given job
		- Reduces employer's cost for training and hiring
	- Unions jobs offer job training and apprenticeship programs
- Firm with union demands may choose production methods that involved more physical capital, and less labour

### The Decline in US Union Membership

- Decline in unions
	- Shift from manufacturing to service industries
	- Force of globalization and increased competition from foreign producers
	- A reduced desire for unions because of the workplace protection laws now in place
	- US legal environment that makes it relatively more difficult for unions to organize workers and expand their membership

- Unions
	- American Federation of State
	- Country and Municipal Employees (AFSCME)
	- Service Employees International Union
	- National Education Association
- US laws are less friendly to the formation of unions than other countries
- National Labour Relations Act
	- Specified that workers had a right to organize unions and that management had to give then a fair chance to do so

## Employment Discrimination

- Barries to equitable participation in the labour market drive down economic growth
- Populations that are underrepresented, underpaid, or mistreated in a labour market or industry, can result in a negative outcome for the larger economy
- Discrimination
	- Acting on the belief that members of a certain group are inferior or deserve less solely because of a factor such as race, gender, or religion

### Earnings Gaps by Race and Gender

<img src="/images/Pasted image 20260404173522.png" alt="image" width="500">

- Not every instance of a wage gap or employment inequity is a product of overt distrimination
	- Issues in society, such as inequitable education or housing segregation, can cause gaps and limitations on economic mobility

### Investigating the Female/Male Earnings Gap

- Aspects of sex-and-gender based earning gaps reveal unexpected causes and impacts
- As women's representation increased in the workforce, jobs that became occupied by women experienced wage reductions relative to jobs being done by men
	- Devaluation

### Investigating the Earnings Gap Related to Race and Ethnicity

- Racial gap is related both to continuing differences in education levels and presence of discrimination
- Racial wage gap widen over times
	- Continuing discrimination
- Redlining
	- Intentional and discriminatory withholding of services or products based on race or other factors
### Competitive Markets and Discrimination

- Competitive markets can allow some employers to practice discrimination, it can also provide profit-seeking firms with incentives not to discriminate
- Businesses located in areas with a large minority population
- Discriminatory impulses can emerge at a number of levels
	- Managers
	- Workers
	- Customers
- "Prejudiced customer"
	- Rationale falls apart when considering the many jobs that have no customer contact

### Public Policies to Reduce Discrimination

- Equal Pay Act (1963)
	- Employers must pay men and women who do equal work the same
- Civil Rights Act (1964)
	- Prohibits employment discrimination based on race, colour, religion, sex, or national origin
- Age Discrimination in Employment Act (1967)
	- Prohibited discrimination based on age
- Civil Rights Act (1991)
	- Provides monetary damages in cases of intentional employment discrimination
- Pregnancy Discrimination
	- Prohibits discrimination against people in the workplace who are planning pregnancy, are pregnant, or returning after pregnancy
- Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
	- Investigates employment discrimination and protect workers who filed complaints against employers
		- Reduced gender wage gap
- Affirmative action
	- Efforts by government or businesses that give special rights to minorities in hiring and promoting to make up the past discrimination

### An Increasingly Diverse Workforce

- Growing proportions of minority workers will break down remaining discriminatory barriers
- Social tensions between genders and ethic groups will rise and that workers will be less productive as a result

## Immigration

### Historical Patterns of Immigration

- Level of immigration has been rising
- Immigrant to the US from Europe where more than 90% of the total during the first decade of the 20th century
- By 2000s, 1/2 of US immigration cam from the rest of the Americas, and 1/4 from Asia

### Economic Effects of Immigration

- Immigrants can contribute to both increased production and increased consumption
- 1/3 of immigrants over the age of 25 lack a high school diploma
- These causes a shift to the right in unskilled labour, and lower wages in these jobs
- Disruptive effect
	- As immigration weighted toward low-skill workers reduces low-skill wages
	- Increased demand for local goods and services
	- Production processes may favour labour intensive low skill jobs
	- Impact on state and local government budgets
		- Increased cost in state-run programs
			- Public schooling
			- Welfare benefits
- The effect of immigration on taxes are generally positive at the federal level, but negative at the state and local levels

### Proposals for Immigration Reform

- Congressional Jordan Commission (1990s)
	- Reducing overall levels of immigration and refocusing on US immigration policy to give priority to immigrants with higher skill levels
