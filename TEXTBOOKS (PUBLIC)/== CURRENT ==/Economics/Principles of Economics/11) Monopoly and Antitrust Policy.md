## Corporate Mergers

- Merger
	- When two formerly separate firms combine to become a single firm
- Acquisition
	- When one firm purchases another

### Regulations for Approving Merges

- Firms that are above a minimum size are required to notify the US Federal Trade Commission (FTC) before a. merger or acquisition
- Antitrust laws
	- The laws that give government the power to block certain mergers, and break up large firms into smaller ones
- The merger will be allowed if the firm agrees to sell of certain parts

- Private firms can
	- Expand or reduce production
	- Set the price they choose
	- Open new factories or sales facilities or close them
	- Hire workers or lay them off
	- Start selling new products or stop selling existing ones

- Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)
- Clayton Antitrust Act
- Celler-Kefauver Act

### The 4-Firm Concentration Ratio

- Concentration ratio
	- Measures the combined market share which is accounted for by the largest firms
- High market concentrations can create inefficiencies in an economy
- Market share
	- Each firm's proportion of total sales in the market

### Herfindahl-Hirschman Index

- Herfindahl Hirschman Index (HHI)
	- Another approach to measure industry concentration
	- Sum of squares of the market share of each firm in the industry
	- $N^2$
- HHI gives greater weight to large firms

	- Case-by-case analysis
### New Directions for Anti-Trust

- 4 firm concentration and HHI assume that the market is well defined
	- Based on implicit assumptions that competitive conditions across industries are similar
- Market
	- Defined as technology or globalization
- Regulators look at detailed evidence on profits and prices for specific stores in different cities to determine if competitive stores were able to enter or exit
- Use of statistical tools and real-world evidence to estimate the demand and supply curve for the proposed merger
- Estimates the likely outcome for consumers if two firms are able to merge

## Regulating Anti-competitive Behaviour

- Illegal for competitors to form a cartel to collude to make pricing and output decisions, as if they were a monopoly firm
- FTC and US DOJ prohibit firms from agreeing to fix prices or output, and sharing or dividing markets by allocating customers, suppliers, territories, of line of commerce

### Restrictive Practices

- Restrictive practices
	- Practices that do not involve outright agreements to raise price or to reduce he quantity produced
	- Might have an effect of reducing competition
- Exclusive dealing
	- Agreement between a manufacturer and dealer can be legal or illegal
		- Legal if the purpose of the contract is to encourage competition
- Tying sales
	- A customer is allowed to buy one product only if the customer also buys a second product
- Bundling
	- A firm sells two or more products as one
		- Cable companies

- Competitors who are losing out in the market can accuse successful firms of anticompetitive restrictive practices

## Regulating Natural Monopolies

- Natural monopoly
	- Average costs are declining over the range of production that satisfies market demand
	- Fixed costs are large relative to variable costs
		- Public utilities

### Cost-Plus vs. Price Cap Regulation

- Regulators of public utilities have followed the general approach to choose a point like F
- Calculate the average cost of production, added the normal rate of profit, and set the price for consumers
	- Cost-plus regulation
- Firms have an incentive to generate high costs because what they can charge is linked to the costs they incur
- Price cap regulation
	- Regulator sets a price that the firm can change over the next few years
	- Require a price that declined slightly over time

## The Great Deregulation Experiment

### Doubts about Regulation of Prices and Quantities

- Deregulation
	- Removing government controls over prices and quantities produced in airlines, railroads, trucking, intercity bus travel, natural gas, and bank interest rates

- Deregulation reduced airfares by 1/3 over the next 2 decades
- Regulatory capture
	- The firms that are supposedly regulated end up playing a large role in setting the regulations that they will follow

### The Effects of Deregulation

- Sarbanes Oxley Act (2002)
	- US federal law to restore investor confidence following major corporate accounting scandals
	- Strict financial resorting, internal controls, and auditing reforms for public companies with criminal penalties
- Dodd Frank Act
	- US federal law
	- Prevent future crises by reducing risks, limiting bank behaviours, and creating the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)
